A Great Loss

by J. Brian Shumate
January 11 2015

The technology community and whole world lost an amazing person two years ago today…

Aaron Swartz • November 8, 1986 – January 11, 2013

I did not know Aaron very well at all; There were but a few exchanges of communication between us in email and on Twitter.

One time, he mentioned that ᏣᎳᎩ was perhaps his favorite name for a language.

Being that I strongly agreed and had recently gotten the letters ᏣᎳᎩ tattooed on my body, I responded:

@aaronsw Me too, but it's in my blood & inked on my arm. :-)

— 42-Byte Nonce (@brianshumate) March 25, 2011

He then asked me for a pic:

@brianshumate pic?

— Aaron Swartz (@aaronsw) March 25, 2011

It was a typical brief exchange on Twitter. I’d also expressed interest in some of his personal projects and working with him on those, but we’d never really managed to make that happen.

Thanks for everything

Aaron was an exceptional human being who exerted incredible amounts of energy in the true hacker spirit while pursuing a never-ending quest for knowledge. He also made some significant changes in this world.

Here are but a few things he did or helped to do:

  • He was involved in the development of RSS
  • He was involved in the development of Markdown
  • He helped organize Creative Commons
  • He created web.py
  • He helped to build reddit
  • He founded Demand Progress
  • He was instrumental in helping to defeat SOPA

This is of course, a fantastic track record and a list of absolutely amazing contributions for a person of such a young age.

If you are not familiar with the life of Aaron Swartz, you can learn a good deal about him by watching the documentary The Internet’s Own Boy.

Missed

Aaron is missed by many and the list of those who actually knew him well is quite long. There is no telling what he could have accomplished if he could have remained here with us, and I think that we all wonder about that a lot. It is unfortunate, sad, and most tragic that he was bullied by our government here in the United States until he apparently could no longer live a peaceful existence.

I am often saddened by the loss of Aaron to his family and to our greater community of nerds, and perhaps selfishly upset that I could not have known him better.

Most of all, I am sad for the loss of such potential he could have continued to share with and for a better world, but I am always happy that he ever existed at all.

Rest in peace, Aaron.
